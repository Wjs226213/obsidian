# The Mind Behind Linux — A TED Interview

**Interviewer:** Chris Anderson (CA) · **Guest:** Linus Torvalds (LT)

---

---

**Chris Anderson:** This is such a strange thing. Your software, Linux, is in millions of computers, it probably powers much of the Internet. And I think that there are, like, a billion and a half active Android devices out there. Your software is in every single one of them. It's kind of amazing. You must have some amazing software headquarters driving all this. That's what I thought — and I was shocked when I saw a picture of it. I mean, this is — this is the Linux world headquarters.

> **Chris Anderson：** 这真是一件奇特的事情。你的软件 Linux 运行在数百万台电脑上，它很可能支撑着互联网的大部分运行。而且我觉得，全球大约有 15 亿部活跃的安卓设备。你的软件在每一台里面。这真了不起。你一定有一个了不起的软件总部在推动这一切。我也是这么想的——但当我看到它的照片时，我震惊了。我是说，这就是——这就是 Linux 的世界总部。

*(Laughter / 笑声)*
*(Applause / 掌声)*

---

**Linus Torvalds:** It really doesn't look like much. And I have to say, the most interesting part in this picture, that people mostly react to, is the walking desk. It is the most interesting part in my office and I'm not actually using it anymore. And I think the two things are related. The way I work is ... I want to not have external stimulation. You can kind of see, on the walls are this light green. I'm told that at mental institutions they use that on the walls.

> **Linus Torvalds：** 它看起来确实不怎么样。而且我得说，这张照片里最有趣的部分，也是大家反应最强烈的，是那个行走式办公桌。它是我办公室里最有趣的物件，但我实际上已经不再用它了。而且我觉得这两件事是相关的。我的工作方式……我不想要外部刺激。你可以看到，墙上是一种浅绿色。有人告诉我，精神病院用的就是这种颜色。

*(Laughter / 笑声)*

---

**LT:** It's like a calming color, it's not something that really stimulates you. What you can't see is the computer here, you only see the screen, but the main thing I worry about in my computer is — it doesn't have to be big and powerful, although I like that — it really has to be completely silent. I know people who work for Google and they have their own small data center at home, and I don't do that. My office is the most boring office you'll ever see. And I sit there alone in the quiet. If the cat comes up, it sits in my lap. And I want to hear the cat purring, not the sound of the fans in the computer.

> **LT：** 这是一种让人平静的颜色，不是那种会刺激你的颜色。你看不到的是这里的电脑，你只能看到屏幕，但我对电脑最在意的是——它不必又大又强大，虽然我喜欢那样——但它必须完全静音。我认识一些在谷歌工作的人，他们在家里有自己的小型数据中心，而我没有。我的办公室是你见过的最无聊的办公室。我独自安静地坐在那里。如果猫过来了，它会趴在我腿上。我想听到猫的呼噜声，而不是电脑风扇的声音。

---

**CA:** So this is astonishing, because working this way, you're able to run this vast technology empire — it is an empire — so that's an amazing testament to the power of open source. Tell us how you got to understand open source and how it lead to the development of Linux.

> **CA：** 这太惊人了，因为你以这种方式工作，却能运营着这个庞大的技术帝国——它确实是一个帝国——这充分证明了开源的力量。请告诉我们，你是如何理解开源的，以及它如何促成了 Linux 的发展。

---

**LT:** I mean, I still work alone. Really — I work alone in my house, often in my bathrobe. When a photographer shows up, I dress up, so I have clothes on.

> **LT：** 其实我现在还是独自工作。真的——我独自在家工作，常常穿着浴袍。当摄影师来的时候，我会穿上正式的衣服，所以照片里我是穿着衣服的。

*(Laughter / 笑声)*

---

**LT:** And that's how I've always worked. I mean, this was how I started Linux, too. I did not start Linux as a collaborative project. I started it as one in a series of many projects I had done at the time for myself, partly because I needed the end result, but even more because I just enjoyed programming. So it was about the end of the journey, which, 25 years later, we still have not reached. But it was really about the fact that I was looking for a project on my own and there was no open source, really, on my radar at all. And what happened is ... the project grows and becomes something you want to show off to people. Really, this is more of a, "Wow, look at what I did!" And trust me — it was not that great back then.

> **LT：** 我一直都是这样工作的。其实，我当初开始做 Linux 的时候也是如此。我并不是以一个协作项目的形式启动 Linux 的。它只是我当时为自己做的众多项目中的一个，部分原因是我需要最终的结果，但更多的是因为我单纯享受编程。所以这关乎旅程的终点——25 年后，我们仍未抵达。但更重要的是，我只是在寻找一个属于自己的项目，当时开源根本不在我的视野里。后来发生的是……这个项目逐渐成长，变成了一件你想向别人炫耀的东西。真的，更像是"哇，看看我做了什么！"相信我——那时候它并没有那么了不起。

---

**LT:** I made it publicly available, and it wasn't even open source at that point. At that point it was source that was open, but there was no intention behind using the kind of open-source methodology that we think of today to improve it. It was more like, "Look, I've been working on this for half a year, I'd love to have comments." And other people approached me. At the University of Helsinki, I had a friend who was one of the open source — it was called mainly "free software" back then — and he actually introduced me to the notion that, hey, you can use these open-source licenses that had been around. And I thought about it for a while. I was actually worried about the whole commercial interests coming in. I mean, that's one of the worries I think most people who start out have, is that they worry about somebody taking advantage of their work, right? And I decided, "What the hell?"

> **LT：** 我把它公开发布了，但当时它甚至不是开源的。那时只是源代码开放了，但我并没有打算用我们今天所说的那种开源方法论来改进它。更像是"看，我花了半年做这个，我希望得到一些反馈。"然后其他人找到了我。在赫尔辛基大学，我有一个朋友是开源——那时候主要叫"自由软件"——他向我介绍了这个理念：嘿，你可以用那些已经存在的开源许可证。我想了一段时间。我其实担心商业利益的介入。我觉得这是大多数刚开始的人都会有的担忧——他们担心别人占用自己的劳动成果，对吧？最后我决定："管它呢！"

---

**CA:** And then at some point, someone contributed some code that you thought, "Wow, that really is interesting, I would not have thought of that. This could actually improve this."

> **CA：** 然后在某个时候，有人贡献了一些代码，你心想："哇，这真的很有趣，我根本没想到。这确实能改进这个项目。"

---

**LT:** It didn't even start by people contributing code, it was more that people started contributing ideas. And just the fact that somebody else takes a look at your project — and I'm sure it's true of other things, too, but it's definitely true in code — is that somebody else takes an interest in your code, looks at it enough to actually give you feedback and give you ideas. That was a huge thing for me. I was 21 at the time, so I was young, but I had already programmed for half my life, basically. And every project before that had been completely personal and it was a revelation when people just started commenting, started giving feedback on your code. And even before they started giving code back, that was, I think, one of the big moments where I said, "I love other people!"

> **LT：** 甚至不是从人们贡献代码开始的，更多的是人们开始贡献想法。仅仅是有别人看了你的项目——我相信其他领域也是如此，但在代码领域尤其如此——有人对你的代码产生了兴趣，认真地看了它，然后给你反馈和想法。这对我来说意义重大。我当时 21 岁，还很年轻，但我已经编程了大半辈子。在那之前，每一个项目都完全是我个人的，当人们开始评论、开始对你的代码给出反馈时，那对我来说是一种启示。甚至在人们开始回馈代码之前，我认为那已经是一个重大的时刻，我当时说："我爱其他人！"

---

**LT:** Don't get me wrong — I'm actually not a people person.

> **LT：** 别误会——我其实不是一个擅长社交的人。

*(Laughter / 笑声)*

---

**LT:** I don't really love other people —

> **LT：** 我并不真的那么爱别人——

*(Laughter / 笑声)*

---

**LT:** But I love computers, I love interacting with other people on email, because it kind of gives you that buffer. But I do love other people who comment and get involved in my project. And it made it so much more.

> **LT：** 但我热爱电脑，我喜欢通过电子邮件与他人交流，因为它给你提供了一个缓冲。但我确实爱那些评论并参与到我项目中的人。这让一切变得如此不同。

---

**CA:** So was there a moment when you saw what was being built and it suddenly started taking off, and you thought, "Wait a sec, this actually could be something huge, not just a personal project that I'm getting nice feedback on, but a kind of explosive development in the whole technology world"?

> **CA：** 那么有没有这样一个时刻——当你看到正在构建的东西突然开始腾飞，你心想："等等，这真的可能变成一件大事，不再只是一个收到良好反馈的个人项目，而是整个科技世界的一种爆炸性发展"？

---

**LT:** Not really. I mean, the big point for me, really, was not when it was becoming huge, it was when it was becoming little. The big point for me was not being alone and having 10, maybe 100 people being involved — that was a big point. Then everything else was very gradual. Going from 100 people to a million people is not a big deal — to me. Well, I mean, maybe it is if you're —

> **LT：** 并没有。对我来说，重要的时刻不是它变得庞大的时候，而是它刚刚开始有起色的时候。对我来说重要的是不再孤单，有 10 个、也许 100 个人参与进来——那才是重要的时刻。之后的一切都是渐进的。从100人到100万人不是什么大事——对我来说。嗯，我的意思是，也许对你来说是——

*(Laughter / 笑声)*

---

**LT:** If you want to sell your result then it's a huge deal — don't get me wrong. But if you're interested in the technology and you're interested in the project, the big part was getting the community. Then the community grew gradually. And there's actually not a single point where I went like, "Wow, that just took off!" because it — I mean — it took a long time, relatively.

> **LT：** 如果你想卖掉你的成果，那当然是一件大事——别误会。但如果你对技术感兴趣，对这个项目感兴趣，那么最重要的是建立起社区。然后社区逐渐壮大。实际上并没有一个确切的时刻让我觉得"哇，它就这么起飞了！"因为它——我的意思是——相对而言花了很长时间。

---

**CA:** So all the technologists that I talk to really credit you with massively changing their work. And it's not just Linux, it's this thing called Git, which is this management system for software development. Tell us briefly about that and your role in that.

> **CA：** 我采访过的所有技术人员都认为你极大地改变了他们的工作方式。而且不仅仅是 Linux，还有这个叫 Git 的东西，它是一个软件开发管理系统。请简单地跟我们讲讲，以及你在这个项目中的角色。

---

**LT:** So one of the issues we had, and this took a while to start to appear, is when you ... When you grow from having 10 people or 100 people working on a project to having 10,000 people, which — I mean, right now we're in the situation where just on the kernel, we have 1,000 people involved in every single release and that's every two months, roughly two or three months. Some of those people don't do a lot. There's a lot of people who make small, small changes. But to maintain this, the scale changes how you have to maintain it. And we went through a lot of pain. And there are whole projects that do only source-code maintenance. CVS is the one that used to be the most commonly used, and I hated CVS with a passion and refused to touch it and tried something else that was radical and interesting and everybody else hated.

> **LT：** 我们遇到的一个问题——这个问题过了一段时间才显现出来——就是当你……当你从一个项目有10个人或100个人参与，发展到有1万人的时候——我是说，现在仅仅在内核上，每个版本就有1000人参与，而每两到三个月就发布一个版本。有些人做的并不多。很多人只做很小的改动。但要维持这个规模，就必须改变维护方式。我们经历了很多痛苦。有些项目专门做源代码维护。CVS 曾经是最常用的，但我极其讨厌 CVS，拒绝使用它，然后试了另一种激进有趣但其他人都不喜欢的东西。

---

**CA:** (Laughs)
**CA：**（笑）

---

**LT:** And we were in this bad spot, where we had thousands of people who wanted to participate, but in many ways, I was the kind of break point, where I could not scale to the point where I could work with thousands of people. So Git is my second big project, which was only created for me to maintain my first big project. And this is literally how I work. I don't code for — well, I do code for fun — but I want to code for something meaningful so every single project I've ever done has been something I needed and —

> **LT：** 我们陷入了一个困境：有成千上万的人想要参与，但在很多方面，我成了那个瓶颈，我无法扩大到能跟上千人一起工作的规模。所以 Git 是我的第二个大项目，它的创建仅仅是为了让我能够维护我的第一个大项目。这完全就是我的工作方式。我写代码不是为了——好吧，我确实为了乐趣而写代码——但我希望写代码是为了有意义的事情，所以我做过的每一个项目都是我自己需要的东西——

---

**CA:** So really, both Linux and Git kind of arose almost as an unintended consequence of your desire not to have to work with too many people.

> **CA：** 所以实际上，Linux 和 Git 几乎都是你不想和太多人一起工作这一愿望的意外产物。

---

**LT:** Absolutely. Yes.
> **LT：** 完全正确。是的。

*(Laughter / 笑声)*

---

**CA:** That's amazing.
**LT:** Yeah.

> **CA：** 这太神奇了。
> **LT：** 是啊。

*(Applause / 掌声)*

---

**CA:** And yet, you're the man who's transformed technology not just once but twice, and we have to try and understand why it is. You've given us some clues, but ... Here's a picture of you as a kid, with a Rubik's Cube. You mentioned that you've been programming since you were like 10 or 11, half your life. Were you this sort of computer genius, you know, übernerd, were you the star at school who could do everything? What were you like as a kid?

> **CA：** 但你是那个改变了技术世界的人，而且不止一次，是两次。我们必须试着理解这是为什么。你已经给了一些线索，但是……这是一张你小时候的照片，拿着一个魔方。你提到你从10岁或11岁就开始编程，那是你半辈子的时间。你是那种电脑天才，超级极客吗？你是学校里什么都能做的明星吗？你小时候是什么样的人？

---

**LT:** Yeah, I think I was the prototypical nerd. I mean, I was ... I was not a people person back then. That's my younger brother. I was clearly more interested in the Rubik's Cube than my younger brother.

> **LT：** 是的，我觉得我是典型的书呆子。我是说，我……那时候我就不擅长社交。那是我弟弟。我显然对魔方比对我弟弟更感兴趣。

*(Laughter / 笑声)*

---

**LT:** My younger sister, who's not in the picture, when we had family meetings — and it's not a huge family, but I have, like, a couple of cousins — she would prep me beforehand. Like, before I stepped into the room she would say, "OK. That's so-and-so ..." Because I was not — I was a geek. I was into computers, I was into math, I was into physics. I was good at that. I don't think I was particularly exceptional. Apparently, my sister said that my biggest exceptional quality was that I would not let go.

> **LT：** 我的妹妹，照片里没有她。当我们有家庭聚会时——我们家不算大，但我有几个堂表兄妹——她会提前帮我做准备。就像在我走进房间之前，她会说："好的，那是某某某……"因为我不是——我是个极客。我沉迷电脑，沉迷数学，沉迷物理。我擅长那些。我不觉得自己特别出众。据我妹妹说，我最大的特别之处是**我不放手**。

---

**CA:** OK, so let's go there, because that's interesting. You would not let go. So that's not about being a geek and being smart, that's about being ... stubborn?

> **CA：** 好，那我们来谈谈这一点，因为很有意思。你不放手。所以那不是关于极客和聪明，而是关于……固执？

---

**LT:** That's about being stubborn. That's about, like, just starting something and not saying, "OK, I'm done, let's do something else — Look: shiny!" And I notice that in many other parts in my life, too. I lived in Silicon Valley for seven years. And I worked for the same company, in Silicon Valley, for the whole time. That is unheard of. That's not how Silicon Valley works. The whole point of Silicon Valley is that people jump between jobs to kind of mix up the pot. And that's not the kind of person I am.

> **LT：** 那是关于固执。就是开始做一件事，然后不会说："好了，我做完了，去做点别的吧——看，那个更闪亮！"我发现在我生活的其他方面也是如此。我在硅谷住了七年。那七年里我一直为同一家公司工作。这简直是闻所未闻。硅谷不是这样的。硅谷的意义就在于人们跳槽，像搅拌大锅一样。但我就不是那种人。

---

**CA:** But during the actual development of Linux itself, that stubbornness sometimes brought you in conflict with other people. Talk about that a bit. Was that essential to sort of maintain the quality of what was being built? How would you describe what happened?

> **CA：** 但是在 Linux 本身的开发过程中，这种固执有时会让你与他人产生冲突。谈谈这个吧。这对保持正在构建的东西的质量来说是必要的吗？你会怎么描述当时的情况？

---

**LT:** I don't know if it's essential. Going back to the "I'm not a people person," — sometimes I'm also ... shall we say, "myopic" when it comes to other people's feelings, and that sometimes makes you say things that hurt other people. And I'm not proud of that.

> **LT：** 我不知道这是不是必要的。回到"我不擅长社交"这一点——有时候我也可以说是……在顾及他人感受方面有些"短视"，这有时会让你说出伤害别人的话。我对此并不自豪。

*(Applause / 掌声)*

---

**LT:** But, at the same time, it's — I get people who tell me that I should be nice. And then when I try to explain to them that maybe you're nice, maybe you should be more aggressive, they see that as me being not nice.

> **LT：** 但同时——总有人告诉我要友善。然后当我试图向他们解释：也许你友善，也许你该更激进一些——他们就会把这看作是我的不友善。

*(Laughter / 笑声)*

---

**LT:** What I'm trying to say is we are different. I'm not a people person; it's not something I'm particularly proud of, but it's part of me. And one of the things I really like about open source is it really allows different people to work together. We don't have to like each other — and sometimes we really don't like each other. Really — I mean, there are very, very heated arguments. But you can, actually, you can find things that — you don't even agree to disagree, it's just that you're interested in really different things.

> **LT：** 我想说的是，我们是不一样的。我不是一个擅长社交的人；这不是我特别引以为豪的事，但它是我的一部分。我非常喜欢开源的一点是，它真正允许不同的人一起工作。我们不必喜欢彼此——有时候我们确实不喜欢彼此。真的——我是说，会有非常激烈的争论。但实际上，你可以找到一些东西——你们甚至不需要"求同存异"，只是你们感兴趣的事情完全不同而已。

---

**LT:** And coming back to the point where I said earlier that I was afraid of commercial people taking advantage of your work, it turned out, and very quickly turned out, that those commercial people were lovely, lovely people. And they did all the things that I was not at all interested in doing, and they had completely different goals. And they used open source in ways that I just did not want to go. But because it was open source they could do it, and it actually works really beautifully together. And I actually think it works the same way. You need to have the people-people, the communicators, the warm and friendly people who like —

> **LT：** 回到我之前说的，我担心商业人士会占你劳动的便宜，但结果证明——而且很快证明——那些商业人士是非常非常可爱的人。他们做了所有我完全不感兴趣的事情，他们有完全不同的目标。他们以我根本不想走的方式使用开源。但因为那是开源的，他们可以这么做，而且实际上配合得非常完美。我真的认为事情就是这样运作的。

---

*(Laughter / 笑声)*

---

**LT:** ...really want to hug you and get you into the community. But that's not everybody. And that's not me. I care about the technology. There are people who care about the UI. I can't do UI to save my life. I mean, if I was stranded on an island and the only way to get off that island was to make a pretty UI, I'd die there.

> **LT：** ……那些真正想拥抱你、把你拉进社区的人。但并不是所有人都是那样。我也不是那样的人。我关心的是技术。有些人关心的是用户界面。我完全做不了 UI，打死我也做不了。我是说，如果我在一个荒岛上，唯一离开的方式是做一个漂亮的用户界面，那我宁愿死在那。

*(Laughter / 笑声)*

---

**LT:** So there's different kinds of people, and I'm not making excuses, I'm trying to explain.

> **LT：** 所以有不同种类的人，我不是在找借口，我是在试图解释。

---

**CA:** Now, when we talked last week, you talked about some other trait that you have, which I found really interesting. It's this idea called taste. And I've just got a couple of images here. I think this is an example of not particularly good taste in code, and this one is better taste, which one can immediately see. What is the difference between these two?

> **CA：** 上周我们谈话时，你谈到了你的另一个特质，我觉得非常有意思。就是那个叫"品味"的概念。我这里有两张图片，这一张是代码品味不太好的例子，这一张是品味更好的，一眼就能看出来。这两者的区别是什么？

---

**LT:** So this is — How many people here actually have coded?

> **LT：** 这个——在座有多少人真的写过代码？

---

**CA:** Oh my goodness.
> **CA：** 天哪。

---

**LT:** So I guarantee you, everybody who raised their hand, they have done what's called a singly-linked list. And it's taught — This, the first not very good taste approach, is basically how it's taught to be done when you start out coding. And you don't have to understand the code. The most interesting part to me is the last if statement. Because what happens in a singly-linked list — this is trying to remove an existing entry from a list — and there's a difference between if it's the first entry or whether it's an entry in the middle. Because if it's the first entry, you have to change the pointer to the first entry. If it's in the middle, you have to change the pointer of a previous entry. So they're two completely different cases.

> **LT：** 我敢保证，每一个举手的人都写过所谓的单向链表。教学上——这个，第一个品味不太好的方法，基本上是初学编程时被教导的做法。你不需要理解代码。对我来说最有趣的是最后一个 if 语句。因为在单向链表中——这个操作是从列表中删除一个现有的条目——如果是第一个条目和如果是中间的条目，情况是不同的。因为如果是第一个条目，你需要改变指向第一个条目的指针。如果是中间的条目，你需要改变前一个条目的指针。所以这是两种完全不同的情况。

**CA:** And that's better.
> **CA：** 而那个更好。

And it doesn't really matter — I don't want you understand why it doesn't have the if statement, but I want you to understand that sometimes you can see a problem in a different way and rewrite it so that a special case goes away and becomes the normal case. And that's good code. But this is simple code. This is CS 101. This is not important — although, details are important.

> **LT：** 而这个更好。它没有那个 if 语句。这不重要——我不需要你理解为什么没有那个 if 语句，但我希望你能理解：有时候你可以用不同的方式看待一个问题，然后重写它，让一个特殊情况消失，变成正常情况。这就是好的代码。但这是简单的代码。这是计算机科学基础。这不重要——虽然细节很重要。

> **LT：** 对我来说，我真正想与之共事的人的一个标志就是他们有好的品味。这就是为什么……我给你发了这个愚蠢的例子，但它不相关，因为太小了。好的品味远不止于此。好的品味是能够真正看到大的模式，并且本能地知道什么是做事的正确方式。

> **LT：** 其实过去两天我在 TED 有点不自在，因为这里充满了远见卓识，对吧？但我不是一个有远见的人。我没有五年计划。我是一个工程师。我真的觉得——我是说——我对所有那些仰望星空、凝视云朵、说"我想去那里"的人感到很高兴。但我看着地面，我想在掉进去之前，先把眼前的坑填了。我就是这样的人。

> **LT：** 关于这个问题，我有两种想法。我认为开源在代码领域如此成功的一个原因是，归根结底，代码往往是黑白分明的。通常有很好的方式来判断：这个做对了，这个做得不好。代码要么能运行，要么不能，这意味着争论的空间比较小。尽管这样，我们还是有争论，对吧？在很多其他领域——我是说，人们谈论过开放式政治之类的东西——有时候很难说你可以把同样的原则应用到其他领域，因为黑白不仅变成了灰色，还变成了不同的颜色。
